Hypotheses

We assume that the data consist of independent random samples of size n1 and n2 from two normal populations,

Group 1:   Y   ~   normal (µ1 , σ1)
Group 2:   Y   ~   normal (µ2 , σ2)

We will not consider whether the two means, µ1 and µ2, are the same at present. (A t-test for this hypothesis was described earlier.) Instead, we only examine whether the standard deviations (or equivalently variances) are equal,

Test statistic

The test statistic for assessing these hypotheses is based on the sample standard deviations in the two groups, s1 and s2.

Difference?
It might initially be thought that the difference between the two standard deviations (or the two variances) could be used as the basis of a test statistic. However the difference does not have a standard distribution. Also, linear transformation of the data (e.g. translating temperatures from degrees Celsius to Farhrenheit) alters the test statistic.
Ratio?
A better test statistic is obtained from the ratio of the two standard deviations. Its square is the ratio of the two sample variances and is called an F ratio.

This test statistic is invariant to linear transformations of the sample data. For example, changing temperatures from degrees Celsius to Farhrenheit results in the same F ratio.

F distribution

Provided the data are sampled from normal populations, the F ratio has a standard distribution called an F distribution. The shape of this distribution depends on the sample sizes of the two groups — the F distribution has two parameters called its 'degrees of freedom' that are one less than the sample sizes in the groups. Use the sliders to adjust the degrees of freedom for the distribution (one fewer than the sample sizes in the two groups for the F variance-ratio statistic). Observe that:

  • The distribution is skew. (Values cannot be less than zero but can be arbitrarily high.)
  • The distribution is particularly skew when the numerator degrees of freedom are 1 or 2.
  • As the degrees of freedom increase (i.e. the sample sizes increase), the distribution becomes less variable.
  • The value 1.0 is near the centre of the distribution but, because of its skewness, the distribution's mean is higher than 1.0.

In practice, we usually only want to compare variances when both sample sizes are reasonably high.
