Finding a p-value from the t distribution

The p-value for any test is the probability of getting such an 'extreme' test statistic when H0 is true. When testing the value of a population mean, µ, when σ is unknown, the appropriate test statistic is

Since this has a t distribution (with n - 1 degrees of freedom) when H0 is true, the p-value is found from a tail area of this distribution. The relevant tail depends on the alternative hypothesis. For example, if the alternative hypothesis is for low values of µ, the p-value is the low tail area of the t distribution since low values of (and hence t) would support HA over H0.

H0 :   μ  =  μ0
HA :   μ  <  μ0

Two-tailed test

In some hypothesis tests, the alternative hypothesis allows both low and high values of µ.

H0 :   μ  =  μ0
HA :   μ  ≠  μ0
