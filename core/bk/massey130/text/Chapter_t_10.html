Chapter 10   More Exploratory Graphics

10.1   Improved simple graphs

10.1.1   Splitting the stems

For some data sets, a basic stem and leaf plot groups the leaves into too many or too few classes (rows). To improve the presentation, each stem may be repeated either 2 or 5 times.

10.1.2   Histograms with varying class widths

If a histogram has varying class widths, the vertical axis must be 'density'. The histogram shape would be misleading if frequency or relative frequency was used for the vertical axis.

10.1.3   Histogram areas and proportions

The proportion of values in any classes always equals the proportion of the total histogram area that is above the classes.

10.1.4   Displaying outliers

The basic box plot is often modified to display outliers as separate crosses.

10.1.5   Clusters

Box plots cannot show clusters, so must never be used for data with clusters.

10.1.6   Pareto diagrams

If the categories in a bar chart have no natural ordering, it is often helpful to sort the categories into decreasing frequencies. If cumulative frequencies are also shown, the bar chart is called a Pareto diagram.

10.2   Transformations

10.2.1   Linear transformations

Linear transformations of data affect the scale on the axis of graphical displays, but do not otherwise change the shape of the distribution of values.

10.2.2   Log transformations

Nonlinear transformations change the shape of the distribution of values more profoundly. A logarithmic transformation can help detect patterns in very skew data sets.

10.2.3   When to use log transform?

Logarithmic transformations are most useful for 'quantity' data that cover several orders of magnitude.

10.2.4   Power transformations

Power transformations are a more flexible family of nonlinear transformations that are useful in data exploration.

10.2.5   Power transforms & skewness

The effect of power transformations on the skewness of data is evident in a wide range of graphical displays.

10.3   Nonlinear relationships

10.3.1   Correlation and nonlinear relationships

The correlation coefficient is only a good measure of the strength of a relationship if the points in a scatterplot are scattered round a straight line, not a curve.

10.3.2   Transformations and correlation

The correlation coefficient does not adequately describe the strength of a nonlinear relationship. Transforming the variables to linearise the relationship helps.

10.3.3   Transformations and models

If a relationship is nonlinear, a linear model can often be fitted to transformed response or explanatory variables.

10.3.4   Quadratic models

An alternative solution to nonlinearity is to fit a quadratic curve the data, again using the principle of least squares.

10.3.5   Dangers of extrapolation

Since the form of a relationship is unknown beyond the range of x-values in the data, it is always dangerous to extrapolate.

10.3.6   Lowess smoothing of scatterplots

A nonlinear relationship can be summarised with a smooth curve based on fitting least squares lines to 'local' points.

10.4   Displaying multivariate data

10.4.1   Representing a third variable

A third numerical variable can be represented in a scatterplot by use of different symbols or colours.

10.4.2   Rotating 3D scatterplots

Three numerical variables can be displayed in a 3-dimensional scatterplot; this may be rotated to help understand the relationships in the data.

10.4.3   Scatterplot matrix and brushing

An array of scatterplots of all pairs of variables is often informative, especially if the scatterplots are dynamically linked.

10.4.4   Brushing example

'Brushing' refers to dynamic highlighting of the same individuals in multiple linked displays.

10.4.5   Slicing

Slicing is another dynamic technique. Only observations within a range of values of one variable (a slice) are displayed in linked displays.
