What you need to know in this chapter

You should concentrate on the following material when studying the chapter about distributions related to the normal distribution.

Remember that the probability (or probability density) functions, means and variances of these distributions are provided in a formula sheet in the exam — you do not need to remember them.

10.1 Chi-squared distribution

You should be able to derive the pdf of a variable defined as the square of a standard normal variable — a χ²(1 df) distribution — and show that it is identical to that of a Gamma(½, ½) distribution. You should also be able to derive the pdf of the sum of k such squared independent standard normal variables — a χ²(k df) distribution. The mean, variance and other properties of the distribution follow from those of the Gamma distribution.

You also need to know how the distribution of the sample variance from a normal distribution is related to a chi-squared distribution (but not prove it) and use this chi-squared distribution to obtain probabilities about sample variances and a confidence interval.

10.2 T distribution

You need to know how the t distribution is defined from a standard normal and an independent chi-squared variable. You should also be able to derive a pivot with a t distribution from the sample mean and variance of an arbitrary normal distribution and explain how this leads to the standard 100-level confidence interval for the normal distribution's mean.

10.3 F distribution

You should know how the F distribution is defined from two independent chi-squared variables. You also need to be able to find a pivot for the ratio of two independent sample variances and use it to evaluate a confidence interval for the ratio between the underlying population variances. You should also be able to prove that a squared variable with a t distribution has an F distribution.
