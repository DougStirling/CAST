Chapter 11   Control Charts

11.1   Total quality management

11.1.1   Introduction

The quality of any process is critically important to its success. It is important to monitor quality and continually try to maintain or improve it.

11.1.2   The statistical process

A cycle of data collection and analysis is a central part of the quest for improvement to systems and processes.

11.1.3   Finding the cause of problems

If a problem is detected in a process, brainstorming and cause-and-effect diagrams can help to determine the cause of the problem.

11.2   Control chart basics

11.2.1   Introduction

Many business and industrial processes are continuously monitored in order to detect problems early.

11.2.2   Run charts

A run chart plots regular measurements from a process against time. Extreme values indicate problems with the process.

11.2.3   Control limits

For most distributions, almost all values are within 3 standard deviations of the mean. Control limits 3s on each side of the sample mean from training data can be used to indicate problems with the process.

11.2.4   Other signals of being out-of-control

Runs of successive values that are s or 2s from the mean can also be used to indicate problems in the process.

11.2.5   False alarms

False alarms occasionally happen even when the process is in control. Several successive triggers give a clearer indication of problems.

11.2.6   Examples of run charts

Control limits are set from variation when a process is under control then applied to future observations.

11.3   Control charts in practice

11.3.1   Control charts based on samples

In practice, regular small samples are selected from a process rather than individual values. Control charts can be based on the mean and range of these samples to separately target the mean level of the process and its variability.

11.3.2   Control charts for means

If individual values have a skew distribution, the ± 3s limits may be exceeded in 2% or more of values. Sample means are closer to normal, so a control chart for means rarely exceeds its control limits when the process is under control.

11.3.3   Control charts for ranges

A control chart for the range of successive samples can indicate increases in process variability.

11.3.4   Control charts for proportions

In many processes we want to monitor a proportion. A control chart for sample proportions can be used.

11.3.5   Final comments

Managers should act only when a process becomes out of control. It is counter-productive to act on common-cause variation.
