Summarizing Data

The initial chapters of this e-book describe statistical methods to explore and summarise a data set. Choosing appropriate methods depends on the structure of the data set — the number and types of its variables.

Categorical variables
Chapter 2 describes graphical methods for looking at data sets with one or two categorical variables.
Numerical variables
Chapter 3 starts with graphical and numerical summaries of a single numerical variable. It then gives methods for describing the relationship between two numerical variables.

Probability

Chapters 2 and 3 deal with data that have already been collected, i.e. the past. We also want to think about what might happen in the future. The next two chapters consider how to make statements about the possible future value a variable might take. This is done using probability.

Categorical variables
Chapter 4 deals with future values of a categorical variable – called an event. For example, will it rain tomorrow?
Numerical variables
Chapter 5 deals with future values of a numerical variable – called a random variable. For example, what will tomorrow’s exchange rate be?

Inference

Inference means making a statement about a population based on data from a sample. To do this honestly, we must take account of randomness — if we collected the data again by repeating an experiment or collecting data from different people, the values would often be different. Here we need to combine the viewpoints of probability (what we expect to happen) and data summary (what actually did happen).

One variable
Chapters 6 and 7 develop the methodology to answer questions about a single numerical or categorical variable (confidence intervals and hypothesis tests).
Two or more groups
Chapter 8 presents confidence intervals and hypothesis tests to compare two or more groups.

Special topics

Relationships between numerical variables
Chapter 9 introduces regression for modelling the relationship between two or more numerical variables.
Time series
Chapter 10 presents ways to summarise patterns in time series data and make forecasts of future values.
Quality management
Chapter 11 introduces some important concepts in managing quality, and shows how regularly collected data can be used to monitor the output of a processes.
