Predicting the response at x

Recall that our point estimate (best guess) for a the response at a particular value of x is

  =  b0 + b1 x

However, this relationship should be used with caution. First, we should only use the least squares line for prediction when the linear model assumptions discussed earlier in this chapter are satisfied. The most important of these to check are:

  1. No outliers or points with high leverage
  2. No curvature

In addition, it is not sensible to use the linear model to predict far outside the range of the x's we have used to fit the model (the training data). This is called extrapolation. When we extrapolate to an area where we have not collected data, we cannot check that the relationship is still linear for these new x-values.

Tree diameter and timber volume

The value of hardwood trees depends on the volume of timber that can be obtained when the trees are harvested. However the volume of timber cannot be easily measured when the tree is standing, so volume is usually estimated from measurements that are easier to make, such as the tree diameter 4.5 feet above ground level. The diagram below plots the cross-sectional area at this height against the volume of timber for 31 black cherry trees that were harvested in the Allegheny National Forest in Pennsylvania.

The relationship seems fairly linear and no outliers are evident in the scatterplot of the original data or the residual plot. The biggest tree has fairly high leverage but its area seems to conform to a linear model, so it is still reasonable to use a linear regression model.

It is therefore valid to use the least squares estimates of the intercept and slope to predict the timber volumes of other black cherry trees from their cross-sectional area at 4.5 feet above ground level.

Note however that we should not use the least squares equation to predict the timber volume of a tree whose cross-sectional area is 4 ft2 since this is extrapolation beyond the range of data that we have collected. Since there is no data for trees with cross-section greater than 2.5 ft2, we have no information about whether the relationship remains linear or has some other shape at higher cross-sections.

Of course the quality of our prediction is affected by the quality of our training data, and the strength of the linear relationship between x and y.
