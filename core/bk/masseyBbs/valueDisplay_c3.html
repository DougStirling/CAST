Crosses instead of digits

In stem and leaf plots, the leaf digits provide information about the precise values that they represent. For most data sets, this detailed information does not help us to understand the distribution of values — we would get the same impression if the leaf digits were replaced by crosses. Such a graphical display is called a stacked dot plot.

Nursing home residents

You are interested in establishing a nursing home for retired people in the USA. Where should it be sited? Information about current nursing home usage in the different states would be useful.

The diagram below is based on a stem and leaf plot of the number per 500 population aged 65 or more who are nursing home residents in each state. The stem 'axis' has been drawn horizontally and displayed in the more conventional form.

Drag with the mouse over the leaves to discover the names of the states. Note that all leaves in the same column have the same 'units' digits (i.e. stem digits).

Select Crosses from the pop-up menu to display the data as a stacked dot plot.

(Unfortunately neither display directly helps your decision on where to site the new nursing home. Should you pick the state with the lowest number of nursing home residents since there is a lack of facilities? Or would that be the worst choice since elderly people in the state do not seem to like using nursing homes? Further information is needed!)

Stacked dot plots

Although the use of crosses instead of digits removes some detailed information about the values in a data set, stacked dot plots are more flexible than stem and leaf plots. When a computer draws a stacked dot plot, the stacks are determined only by the size of the crosses used in the plot and may not be 'neat' widths.

For practical data analysis, a stacked dot plot usually displays as much information as a stem and leaf plot.


Survival time of businesses that failed in the previous year

A researcher is interested in how long small businesses stay in operation. As part of the study, 120 businesses that closed down during the previous year were investigated. The last registered director of each company was contacted and asked about how long it had been in business. (The information was obtained from local government sources if nobody from the company could be contacted.)

The stacked dot plot below shows the ages of the 120 businesses.

Use the pop-up menu to change the size of the crosses in the diagram. As the crosses get smaller,

  • The number of stacks increases.
  • The position of each cross on the axis reflects the exact value more accurately.
  • The display becomes more jagged.

The stacked dot plot with large crosses displays information about the distribution of 'years in business' as well as that with smaller crosses.


Jittered dot plots

An alternative to stacking crosses in a dot plot is to position each cross at exactly the position of the value along the axis. This is often adequate for small data sets, but in larger data sets, the crosses will overlap.

An alternative solution to stacking of crosses is to randomly move the crosses perpendicularly to the axis in order to separate them somewhat. This is called jittering the points.

(You should rely on a computer to do the jittering for you, but it could be done by hand by rolling a 6- or 10-sided die for each cross to determine its jittering in millimetres.)

Survival time of businesses

The crosses in the dot plot below shows the ages of the companies with crosses at their exact position along the axis.

Drag the slider to jitter the points. Click the button on the right to change the jittering — i.e. to change the random vertical position of the crosses.

Only enough jittering should be used to separate the high density of crosses — moving the slider about half way is best for the data above. Without jittering, too many crosses overlap to allow us to assess the distribution of values.

Note that the vertical positions of the crosses have no importance — the vertical movement of crosses is 'random' and is only intended to separate overlapping crosses.
