Natural variation

When data are collected, all values are rarely the same and an important role of statistics is to describe and analyse this variation. When data are collected from 'individuals', they often vary considerably.

The natural variability of individuals makes it harder to interpret information from surveys. The example below illustrates this variability with a very small survey.

Yoghurt preferences

Food manufacturers often collect 'preference data' to discover which of several recipes for a type of product is preferred by consumers. In the example below, 30 consumers were each given samples of strawberry yoghurt with three different types of strawberry flavouring, A, B and C. Each consumer reported the flavouring that was preferred.

The percentage preferring flavouring A is shown. Click Another 30 people a few times to repeat the data collection with different groups of 30 people.

The natural variability of the people implies that the proportion preferring each flavouring varies. A full analysis of the data should take account of the variability in the proportion preferring flavouring A.

Explained and unexplained variation

In some types of data, we may be able to 'explain' some of the variation in a measurement in terms of other known characteristics of the individuals. Other variation remains unexplained.

Yoghurt preferences

In the yoghurt preference example above, there may be differences between males and females. If we could tell that a particular flavouring was liked better by females than males, advertising for it could be targeted better at that gender.

Systematic differences in preference between males and females are the 'signal' in the data about which we are most interested.

In this example, variation within each gender is unexplained 'noise' variation that only serves to obscure the signal.


Sometimes data are collected from experiments in which some experimental conditions are intentionally varied.

Intentional differences to experimental conditions may also cause systematic differences in variables.

In many situations, information (signal) can be obscured by random variation (noise). Natural variability makes it harder to interpret experimental results.
