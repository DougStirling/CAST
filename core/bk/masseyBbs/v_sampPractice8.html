Cost and accuracy

Stratified, cluster and two-stage sampling schemes have various advantages and disadvantages, compared to a sample random sample that records information from the same number of individuals.

This page uses simulations to illustrate some of these differences. However these simulations involve ideas that will not be described until much later in this course.

We suggest that you revisit this page after studying Chapter 6 of the e-book.

Stratified sampling

A stratified random sample provides more accurate estimates of population characteristics than a simple random sample of the same size from the population. The advantage of stratified sampling is greatest when the strata differ in their mean value of the characteristic.

We will explain in a later chapter that the accuracy of an estimate is described by its standard deviation. A formula will be given for the standard deviation of the mean from a simple random sample.

The ordinary formula for the standard deviation of a sample mean will under-estimate its accuracy if the mean is obtained from a stratified random sampling.

Weekly turnover by grocery stores

In practice, the aim is more likely to be estimation of the total grocery turnover in the city, but this is simply 100 times the mean turnover, so stratified sampling gives the same improvement over a simple random sample.


Cluster sampling

As mentioned earlier, it is usually much cheaper to obtain information from individuals in clusters than from the same number of individuals in a simple random sample.

However the penalty is that the mean population characteristic is less accurately estimated.

The ordinary formula for the standard deviation of a sample mean will over-estimate its accuracy if the mean is obtained by cluster sampling.

Two-stage sampling

As with cluster sampling, two-stage sampling has lower cost than simple random sampling but estimates population characteristics less accurately.

The number of primary units to sample is therefore a trade-off between accuracy and cost. The details are beyond the scope of CAST.

As with cluster and stratified sampling, formulae for the standard deviation of the resulting estimates are beyond the scope of an introductory course.
