Formulae for mean and standard deviation of binomial distribution

For any binomial distribution with parameters n and π, we could form a table of probabilities for the possible values (0, 1, ..., n). From this table, we could evaluate the mean and standard deviation of the variable using the general methods described in the previous section.

For binomial distributions, we can avoid these calculations with the following compact formulae for a binomial distribution's mean and standard deviation:

E[X]   =   µ   =   nπ

sd(X)   =   σ   =   √ nπ(1 - π)

Example

The table below shows the distribution of a binomial random variable describing the number of successes in n = 5 trials with probability π = 0.3 in each.

  x     P(X = x)  
0 0.16807
1 0.36015
2 0.30870
3 0.13230
4 0.02835
5 0.00243

Using the general formula for expected values,

E[X]   =   0 × 0.16807   +   1 × 0.36015   +   ...   +   5 × 0.00243   =   1.5

This is identical to what can be obtained from the formula,

E[X]   =   nπ   =   5 × 0.3   =   1.5

The formula for the binomial standard deviation also gives the same value that could be obtained by hand,

σ   =   √ nπ(1 - π)   =   √ (5 × 0.3 × 0.7)   =   1.025

Survival of newly planted trees

Eighty percent of the trees planted by a woodlands conservation group survive. If they plant 15 trees in an area, what are the mean and standard deviation of the number of survivors?

Assuming that each tree independently has probability 0.8 of survival, the number of trees surviving has a binomial distribution with parameters n = 15 and π = 0.8. The mean and standard deviation of the number surviving are therefore:

E[X]   =   nπ   =   15 × 0.8   =   12 trees

σ   =   √ nπ(1 - π)   =   √ (15 × 0.8 × 0.2)   =   1.549 trees
