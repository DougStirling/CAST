Standardised residuals

A numerical method that helps us to identify outliers involves calculation of standardised residuals for the data points.

standardised residual  =   e
se

where e is the point's residual and se is an estimate of its standard deviation. Points with standardised residuals that are far from zero lie a long way above or below the regression line, i.e. they are poorly predicted.

If our linear model is correct, each standardised residual will have approximately a standard normal distribution, so we expect that only about 5% will lie outside ±2, and hardly any will lie outside ±3. Most software will automatically identify any points with standardized residuals outside ± 3 for you.

Standadised residual greater than 3 or less than -3 are often taken to indicate possible outliers.

It is worth remembering however that there is still a probability 0.003 that a value from the standard normal distribution will be outside ±3. In large data sets, do not assume that standardized residuals outside ±3 must be outliers. For instance, in a data set of 1000 values, it would be expected that 3 values would be labeled as outliers by this rule.

In large data sets, do not assume that standardised residuals outside ±3 must be outliers — values a little outside can also occur by chance.

Click Another Data Set a few times and observe that standardised residuals are occasionally outside ±2. When the sample size is increased, there are often some standardised residuals outside this range.

It is unusual for standardised residuals to be outside ±3 when the sample size is small, but even this is not uncommon when the sample size is large.

Standardised residuals would need to be outside ±3.5 or ±4 for us to be really confident that they are outliers.
