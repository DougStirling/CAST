Modelling randomness

In order to understand the variability of data, we must find a model that explains why repeating data collection would result in different data values.

A model that explains randomness can also explain the variability (accuracy) of summary statisics such as means.

Samples from a real population

In some data sets, the individuals from whom data were collected were selected as representatives of a larger group of individuals about whom we are really interested.

Opinion polls

Many public opinion polls are conducted to get information about attitudes.

  • Which candidate do you intend to vote for in the coming election?
  • Do you use coffee brand A?
  • How far do you travel to work or school each day?

In these polls, there is usually a well-defined population of interest — all people in the region — but it would be prohibitively expensive to obtain information from everyone. Furthermore, it would be impossible to obtain information from everyone.

Data are usually obtained from a subset of this population.

Simple random samples

There are various different methods of selecting individuals from a population. The simplest of these is a simple random sample. In a simple random sample, each individual has the same chance of being selected.

This mechanism (randomly selecting a sample of n individuals from a larger population) can explain the randomness underlying the observed data.

Shop owners in town

There are 132 shops in a town that are operated by their owners (therefore excluding shops that are part of national chains). The town council wants to find information from these shops by interviewing their owners but has neither money nor time to do this for all shops.

Click Take sample a few times to take simple random samples of 20 shop owners from the 132.

A consequence of random sampling of individuals is that numerical and categorical data collected from the 20 shop owners is also random.

The mean weekly turnover from the 20 sampled owners would not be identical to the unknown population mean from all 132 shops, but random sampling can potentially describe the variability in such a sample mean.

Modelling randomness of data with no concrete population

We use a similar mechanism to model the randomness of data even when the individuals are not selected from any real finite population. We model the process that generates the data using a hypothetical infinite population and treat the data as a simple random sample from this population.

The 'population' can be imagined to be the set of all measurements that would be obtained if data collection was repeated an infinite number of times.

The idea of modelling random data in terms of a hypothetical infinite population is difficult to understand and will be discussed more fully later in this chapter.

Concrete hardness

The hardness of a type of brick paver was investigated by impacting the surface of 10 pavers for 1 minute and recording the volume of material eroded from each.

We model these data as a random sample of 10 values from a hypothetical population that contains the results of repeating the experiment an infinite number of times with different pavers of the same type.

As when sampling from a real finite population, the characteristics of the population are unknown in practical applications.
